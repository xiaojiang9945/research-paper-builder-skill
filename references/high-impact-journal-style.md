# High-Impact Journal Style

Use this reference for Nature, Cell, Science, PNAS, Nature Plants, Molecular Plant, The Plant Cell or any manuscript where broad significance, tight wording and strong editorial triage matter.

## Editorial Premise

High-impact papers must satisfy two readers:
- a broad editor who asks whether the problem and advance matter beyond a narrow subfield;
- a specialist reviewer who asks whether the evidence truly supports the claim.

Write for both. Do not make the language broader than the evidence.

## Broad-Audience Framing

The first abstract sentence and the opening of the Introduction should answer:
- What is the larger problem?
- Why has it remained hard?
- What exact gap does this study close?
- What evidence makes the advance credible?
- Where does the claim stop?

Prefer concrete significance over promotional adjectives. If broad significance is thin, flag it as a structural issue instead of inflating the wording.

## High-Impact Abstract Pattern

Use this scaffold only after the Results are stable:
1. Broad problem in one sentence.
2. Specific bottleneck or missing evidence.
3. "Here, we..." statement naming the study action.
4. Primary result with quantitative or mechanistic support.
5. Secondary result or validation layer.
6. Bounded implication.

Cut secondary claims when the abstract becomes crowded. Do not hide weak evidence behind dense wording.

## Introduction Hourglass

Strong introductions narrow cleanly:
- broad field need;
- current knowledge;
- unresolved bottleneck;
- why this study system can address it;
- exact question or hypothesis;
- brief preview of the supported advance.

The Discussion widens again:
- strongest finding;
- interpretation against prior work;
- field-level implication;
- limitation and next test.

## Sentence Discipline

- Keep sentences short enough to parse on first read.
- Put the actor and finding early.
- Avoid stacked noun phrases when a verb can carry the meaning.
- Use active verbs for direct measurements and cautious verbs for inference.
- Remove filler words that sound high-impact but add no evidence.
- Avoid ornamental contrast framing when a direct positive statement is clearer.

## Claim Calibration

Use:
- `showed`, `increased`, `decreased`, `differed` for direct measurements;
- `was associated with`, `tracked`, `separated` for correlations or grouping;
- `supported`, `was consistent with`, `prioritized` for integrated evidence;
- `suggests`, `may reflect`, `warrants testing` for plausible but unverified mechanisms.

Avoid:
- `proves`, `establishes`, `redefines`, `unprecedented`, `first ever` unless independently defensible;
- field-wide claims from one accession, environment, dataset or model;
- mechanism claims from annotation, enrichment or association alone.

## What To Flag

Flag before polishing if:
- the manuscript has no single central advance;
- the strongest result is a technical screen but the title claims biology;
- validation is external or indirect but the text claims mechanism;
- the display-item count is too high for the target venue;
- the paper is written around analysis chronology rather than evidence logic.
